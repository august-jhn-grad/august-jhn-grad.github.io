Permutations, Set-groups, and Cayley's Theorem

Group action is one of the most important uses of group theory.

Definition. Let \(G\) be a group, let \(S\) be a set. Then a (left) action of \(G\) on \(A\) is a map \[\alpha:S\times G \to S, \] which satisfies for any \(g,h\in G, s\in S\)

  • \(\alpha(s,1) = s\)
  • \(\alpha(\alpha(s,g),h) = \alpha(s,gh)\)


An easy consequence of this definition is that the maps \(\alpha(\_, g):S\to S\) are bijections. To see this, we need only find a left and right inverse. Well, by the first second of the rules which defines actions, and by the very definition of a group (and the existence of a left and right multiplicative inverse \(g^{-1})\), the map \(\alpha(\_,g^{-1})\) must be a left and right inverse: \[\alpha(\alpha(s,g),g^{-1})\ = \alpha(s, gg^{-1}) = \alpha(s,1) = s\] In other words, \(\alpha(\_, g)\in \mathbf{Aut}(G)\).
