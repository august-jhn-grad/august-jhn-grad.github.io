Introduction

A knot is an embeding of the circle \(\mathbb{S}^1\hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3\). While each of these embedings are homeomorphic to each other, they can take on a variety of different shapes. For example, the trivial knot, which could be parametrized as \((cos\theta, \sin\theta), \theta \in [0,2\pi]\). (note that the 3d model of a knot is thickened so as to make it look nice. In reality, knots don't have any girth, and are a 1D object.)

Can you tell the difference? Is there a difference? What would it mean for there to a difference? This is the domain of knot theory, in which these questions find a definate meaning, and a definite answer.

Other questions pertaining to knot theory are:

  • When are two knots the same?
  • How does one prove two knots are the same?
  • How does one represent a knot? (combinatorially, algebraically...)
  • How does one distinguish between knots?
  • Under a reasonable definition of uniqueness, how many knots are there?
We'll explore and find answers to all of these questions, making some remarkable connections to quandles, groups, and racks along the way!
