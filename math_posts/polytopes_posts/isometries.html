Isometries

We start by defining the Euclidean plane. For us, the Euclidean plane is a particular metric space. It is a particular set, with a particular distance function on that set.

Definition. The Euclidean plane is the set \(\mathbb{R}^n\), endowed with the metric \(d\), which is defined for any two points \(\mathbf{p} = (p_1,\dots, p_n), \mathbf{q} = (q_1,\dots, q_n), \) as \[d(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{q}) = \sqrt{\sum_{i = 1}^n (p_i - q_i)^2}.\] This metric space is denoted as \(\mathbb{E}^n = (\mathbb{R}^n , d).\)

Since the underlying set of Euclidean space is a cartesian product, we can also view Euclidean space as a vector space. Every point of Euclidean space is some linear combination of the standard basis vectors \(\mathbf{e}_i\) \[ \mathbf{p} = \sum_{i = 1}^n\mathbf{e}_i \] A morphism of metric spaces is, you guessed it, a distance preserving map from one space to another. Definition. Let \((X, d_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y)\) be metric spaces. An isometry is a function \(f: X\to Y\) such that for any points \(p,q\in X\), \[d_X(p,q) = d_Y(f(p), f(q)).\] An automorphism \(\alpha:X\to X\) is a bijective isometry whose inverse is an isometry. That is, an automorphism of a metric space is an isomorphism \(X\cong X\). As always, the automorphisms form a group. This group is denoted \(\mathbf{Aut}(X)\).

The automorphism group of a metric space tells you much about the space. The isometry group is such a valuable theoretical tool that towards the turn of the century, mathematicians such as Felix Klein suggested that geometry ought to center around the study of such groups.

The group \(\mathbf{Aut}(\mathbb{E}^n)\), for some natural number \(n\), is massive, and rich in structure. I like to think of it as a metropolis of groups, since within each euclidean isometry group lives a great population of other familiar groups. In fact, we'll show that every finite group appears as a subgroup of some euclidean group! The Euclidean group is not just a group: it is a topological group and a Lie group. We will always study this group in relation to the obvious action of \(\mathbf{Aut}(\mathbb{E}^n) \) on the plane. An interesting yet obvious result about the structure of any isometry group will soon be obvious.

Proposition For any natural numbers \(n \le m\), there is a natural inclusion \[\mathbf{Aut}(\mathbb{E}^n) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{Aut}(\mathbb{E}^m)\]
